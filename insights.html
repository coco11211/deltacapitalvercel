    INTERNAL — RESEARCH

    On the Instability of Rolling Covariance

    Rolling covariance matrices, computed over fixed lookback windows, exhibit a pathological sensitivity to the choice of window length. A 60-day window and a 120-day window can produce meaningfully different portfolio allocations on the same date, given the same universe of assets. This is not a technical nuisance. It is a structural feature of non-stationary return series.

    Our observation: the instability is concentrated in the off-diagonal elements. Variance estimates converge relatively quickly. Cross-asset correlations do not. This asymmetry has direct implications for portfolio construction. We now estimate the covariance matrix using a shrinkage estimator that imposes structure on the correlation matrix while leaving variances unconstrained. The reduction in portfolio turnover has been significant.

    The deeper question remains open: if the true covariance structure is regime-dependent, is a single estimator ever sufficient? We suspect not, and are investigating mixture-based approaches that blend multiple covariance estimates, weighted by regime probability.

    INTERNAL — METHODOLOGY

    Regime Detection Under Non-Stationarity

    The standard Hidden Markov Model assumes a fixed number of regimes and stationary transition probabilities. Neither assumption holds in financial markets. Regimes emerge, persist for variable durations, and dissolve. The transition dynamics themselves evolve.

    We have been experimenting with an online changepoint detection framework that does not require pre-specification of regime count. The method operates on a rolling basis, computing the posterior probability that the most recent observation belongs to the current regime versus a new one. When the new-regime probability exceeds a calibrated threshold, the system flags a potential transition.

    Early results are encouraging but imperfect. The framework detects genuine regime shifts with reasonable latency (3–7 trading days) but produces false positives during periods of elevated but directionless volatility. The false positive rate is our current focus. Reducing it without increasing detection latency is a constrained optimization problem in itself.

    INTERNAL — INFRASTRUCTURE

    On the Cost of Precision in Execution

    There is a diminishing return to execution precision. Below a certain latency threshold, the marginal improvement in fill quality is dominated by the infrastructure cost required to achieve it. For our strategy horizons (intraday to multi-week), microsecond optimization is economically irrelevant.

    What matters is not speed but predictability. We optimize for consistent execution quality rather than minimal latency. This means deterministic order routing, predictable queue position, and reliable fill reporting. The execution layer is designed to minimize variance in slippage, not to minimize slippage itself.

    This perspective is unfashionable. The industry narrative rewards speed. We prefer reliability. The distinction matters most during periods of market stress, when fast systems compete for increasingly scarce liquidity and the cost of unpredictable execution can exceed the cost of slightly worse average fills.

    INTERNAL — RESEARCH

    The Decay of Crowded Factors

    A factor that is known to everyone is a factor that is priced by everyone. The academic factor zoo—now exceeding 400 published anomalies—has created a peculiar dynamic: the act of documenting a return predictor accelerates its decay.

    We track factor crowding through multiple lenses: abnormal volume in factor-loading portfolios, compression of cross-sectional dispersion, and correlation among strategies that historically loaded on the same factors. When all three indicators align, the probability of a crowding-driven unwind rises materially.

    Our response is not to avoid well-known factors entirely. It is to condition our exposure on crowding signals. The same factor that is dangerous when crowded can be attractive when abandoned. Timing the cycle of adoption and capitulation is itself a source of edge, though one that resists formalization.
